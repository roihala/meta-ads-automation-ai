## 1. ENTITY EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

In the post-Andromeda era (2025-2026), the meaningful unit of analysis and evaluation has fundamentally shifted. Meta's Andromeda update, rolled out in 2024-2025, represents a foundational change in how ads are delivered, moving from a structure-focused model to a personalized ads retrieval engine [1] [3]. This means the evaluation focus is less on ad set or campaign structure and more on the creative concept itself.

**Hierarchy Level:** Evaluation now primarily happens at the **creative concept** level. Andromeda's AI optimizes ad retrieval, ranking, and personalization using predictive modeling, leveraging diverse creative inputs to maximize performance [1] [3]. Jon Loomer emphasizes that creative diversification is essential, as Meta's delivery systems have more options to work with when provided with diverse creative assets, allowing the AI to optimize at scale [3]. The old "1 ad set = 1 ad" structure is no longer optimal, with a preference for fewer ad sets containing multiple ads to allow Meta's algorithm to optimize more effectively [6].

**Leading vs. Lagging Indicators:**

- **Leading Indicators:** Metrics like **Hook Rate** (3s video views / impressions) and **Thumb-Stop Rate** remain important for initial creative assessment, indicating whether an ad captures attention [5]. However, a low hook rate isn't always bad if the ad is highly relevant to a niche audience [5]. **Click-Through Rate (CTR)** is also a leading indicator of creative effectiveness [10].
- **Lagging Indicators:** **Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)** and **Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)** are crucial lagging indicators, reflecting the ultimate business outcome [4]. These metrics are used to evaluate the overall profitability and efficiency of campaigns after sufficient data has been gathered.

**Hook Rate and Thumb-Stop Rate:** While the 30% folklore number for Hook Rate is outdated, practitioners like Barry Hott suggest that a higher hook rate doesn't always equate to better performance, especially if it sacrifices relevance for broad appeal [5]. The focus is on quality engagement rather than just high numbers. Meta's 2026 updates favor faster hooks and creative diversification [14].

## 2. LEARNING PHASE — CURRENT TRUTH

**"50 conversions in 7 days" rule:** The "50 conversions in 7 days" rule is still accurate in 2025-2026 as a threshold for an ad set or campaign to exit the learning phase [7] [8]. Meta's algorithm requires approximately **50 optimization events within a 7-day period** to gather enough signals to stabilize delivery and exit the learning phase [7] [8].

**Edits that Reset Learning:** Meta has generally tightened its learning phase rules. Significant edits that still reset the learning phase include [7]:

- **Budget changes:** Large increases or decreases (e.g., >20% in a short period).
- **Audience changes:** Significant alterations to targeting parameters.
- **Creative changes:** Swapping out ad creatives or making substantial modifications.
- **Bid strategy changes:** Switching between bid caps, cost caps, or lowest cost.

**Signal of Stabilization Post-Learning:** An entity has stabilized post-learning when it consistently delivers conversions at a predictable CPA/ROAS, and its performance metrics (CTR, CPM) remain relatively stable without significant fluctuations [7]. The key is consistent performance over time, indicating that Meta's algorithm has effectively optimized delivery.

## 3. STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE & DATA SUFFICIENCY

**Statistical Significance:** Professional media buyers in 2025-2026 aim for **95% statistical significance** before declaring a winner in A/B tests or making significant decisions based on ad performance [7]. This ensures that observed performance differences are not due to random chance.

**Data Sufficiency (Learning Phase):** Meta's algorithm requires approximately **50 optimization events within a 7-day period** for an ad set or campaign to gather enough signals to stabilize delivery and exit the learning phase [7] [8]. This threshold is crucial for the algorithm to effectively learn and optimize. The budget for a new ad set should be sufficient to generate these 50 conversions per week. For example, if the target CPA is $20, a minimum daily budget of approximately $143 ($20 CPA \* 50 conversions / 7 days) is needed to achieve the 50 conversions per week goal [7].

**CPA Movement Noise vs. Signal:** While specific confidence intervals for CPA movement are not universally published, the general consensus among practitioners is to observe trends over a sufficient period and volume of data. Sudden, small fluctuations in CPA are often considered noise, especially during the learning phase or with low daily conversion volumes. A common recommendation for initial testing budget is around **3x the target CPA** to get a meaningful signal [6].

## 4. KILL CRITERIA

**Kill Criteria:** In the post-Andromeda era, kill criteria are less about rigid rules and more about recognizing signals of creative fatigue or underperformance in an AI-driven environment. While concrete numbers from practitioners are still evolving, several indicators are used:

- **Declining CTR:** A significant drop in Click-Through Rate (CTR) can signal creative fatigue or audience saturation [12].
- **Rising CPM:** An increasing Cost Per Mille (CPM) without a corresponding increase in conversions often indicates that the ad is becoming less relevant or the audience is fatigued [12].
- **Lack of Conversions:** If an ad set or campaign consistently fails to generate the required 50 optimization events per week to exit the learning phase, it may be underperforming and should be considered for pausing or significant adjustments [7].
- **Days Without a Conversion:** While no universal "days without a conversion" rule exists, practitioners often look at the budget spent relative to the target CPA. If an ad spends 2-3x the target CPA without a conversion, it is often killed or paused for review [6].

## 5. SCALING CRITERIA

**Scaling Criteria:** Identifying a "winner" worth scaling in 2025-2026 involves looking for consistent, profitable performance beyond the learning phase. Key indicators include:

- **Stable CPA/ROAS:** Consistent achievement of target CPA and ROAS after exiting the learning phase [7].
- **High Conversion Volume:** Regularly generating a high volume of conversions at an acceptable cost.
- **Positive Trend in Leading Indicators:** Maintaining strong CTR and Hook Rate, indicating continued creative effectiveness.

**"20% every 2-3 days" rule:** The traditional "20% every 2-3 days" rule for scaling budget is still a cautious approach, but Andromeda has made larger scaling safer due to improved AI optimization [13]. Some practitioners suggest that with a robust creative and stable performance, larger budget increases (e.g., 50-100%) can be attempted, but always with close monitoring [13]. However, reckless scaling without sufficient data and stable performance can still lead to performance degradation [6].

**Vertical vs. Horizontal Scaling:**

- **Vertical Scaling (Budget):** Increasing the budget of existing, well-performing ad sets or campaigns. This is generally preferred when the existing creative and audience are performing strongly and have room for more spend without significant CPA increases [6].
- **Horizontal Scaling (Duplicate):** Duplicating winning ad sets or campaigns. This can be effective for testing new audiences or expanding reach, but it can also lead to audience overlap and increased competition if not managed carefully [6]. The decision often depends on the specific campaign structure and the perceived saturation of the current audience.

## 6. FREQUENCY & AUDIENCE FATIGUE

**Frequency Threshold:** The traditional "Frequency > 3" as a universal signal for audience fatigue is becoming outdated in the Andromeda era [12]. While frequency still matters, Andromeda's better audience matching and creative diversification can mean higher frequency numbers don't always equate to fatigue, especially if the creative is fresh and engaging [3] [12].

**Indicators of Fatigue:** Instead of a rigid frequency number, operators look for a combination of signals:

- **Declining CTR and Conversion Rate:** These are stronger indicators of fatigue than frequency alone [12].
- **Rising CPM/CPA:** Increased costs without proportional results suggest the audience is becoming less responsive [12].
- **Negative Feedback:** Increased negative comments or reactions to ads.

**Andromeda's Impact:** Andromeda's ability to dynamically match creatives to audiences helps mitigate fatigue by showing different ad variations to the same user over time [3]. Creative diversification is therefore crucial to combat fatigue in this new environment [3].

## 7. CREATIVE EVALUATION IN THE ANDROMEDA ERA

**Creative Evaluation:** Andromeda fundamentally changed creative evaluation by automating budget distribution and optimizing for personalized ad delivery [1] [3]. This means:

- **Uneven Budget Distribution:** Andromeda can distribute budget unevenly across creatives within a campaign, making it challenging to assess individual creative performance based solely on spend [3].
- **"Winning Creative" Redefined:** A creative receiving only 5% of spend but showing a high CTR might be a missed opportunity or Meta correctly starving it. The focus shifts from individual creative performance in isolation to how it contributes to the overall campaign objective within the AI's optimization [3]. Creative diversification is key, as Meta's AI needs a variety of assets to optimize effectively [3].
- **Cross-Placement Comparison:** Meta decides the mix across placements (Feed vs. Reels vs. Stories). Operators compare creatives by focusing on their overall contribution to campaign goals, rather than trying to manually optimize for each placement [3]. The emphasis is on providing diverse creative assets and trusting Meta's AI to deliver the right creative to the right person at the right time [3].

## 9. ACCOUNT-LEVEL VS CAMPAIGN-LEVEL EVALUATION

**Distinguishing Underperformance:** When a single campaign underperforms, operators distinguish between "this campaign is bad" and "this account is having a bad week" by looking at broader account trends and historical baselines [6].

- **Rolling Baseline Methodology:** For SaaS ad tools managing ongoing accounts, a rolling baseline methodology is recommended. This involves continuously tracking key metrics (CPA, ROAS, CTR, etc.) over a defined period (e.g., 7-day, 14-day, 30-day rolling averages) to establish a dynamic baseline for normal performance [6]. Significant deviations from this rolling baseline, especially across multiple campaigns or ad sets, would indicate an account-level issue rather than an isolated campaign problem [6].
- **Cross-Campaign Analysis:** Comparing the underperforming campaign against other active campaigns within the same account can help identify if the issue is systemic or campaign-specific. If other campaigns are performing well, it points to a campaign-level issue.
- **External Factors:** Considering external factors like seasonality, market changes, or competitor activity can also help contextualize performance dips.

## 10. WHAT MOST PEOPLE GET WRONG

**Common Evaluation Mistakes & Outdated Rules:**

- **Over-reliance on Narrow Targeting:** Pre-Andromeda, detailed targeting was paramount. Now, broad targeting with strong creatives often outperforms heavily segmented structures, as Meta's AI is more effective at finding relevant audiences [3] [6].
- **Rigid Campaign Structures:** The old "1 ad set = 1 ad" structure is now actively misleading. Meta's algorithm performs better with more data concentrated in fewer ad sets, allowing multiple ads to compete within one ad set [6].
- **Micromanaging Placements:** Manually optimizing for specific placements (e.g., Feed vs. Stories) is less effective. Meta's AI determines the optimal mix, so providing diverse creative assets is more important [3].
- **Ignoring Creative Diversification:** Relying on a single creative or minor variations leads to faster creative fatigue and limits Meta's ability to optimize at scale [3].
- **Misinterpreting Frequency:** Using a rigid "Frequency > 3" as a universal kill signal is outdated. Declining performance metrics are more indicative of fatigue, and Andromeda's better audience matching can mean higher frequency doesn't always equal fatigue [12].
- **Testing for too short a period or with insufficient budget:** Acting on signals before statistical significance is reached or before the learning phase is complete leads to premature optimization and suboptimal results [7].

## Deprecated Rules

- **"1 Ad Set = 1 Ad" Structure:** This is actively misleading in the Andromeda era. Meta's algorithm performs better when multiple ads compete within one ad set, allowing for greater optimization [6].
- **Rigid Funnel Separation (TOFU/MOFU/BOFU):** While conceptual understanding of the funnel remains, rigid separation into distinct campaigns/ad sets is often outdated. Meta's AI can now automatically distribute ads across funnel stages within the same ad set, optimizing based on current emotional state rather than rigid stages [6].
- **Micromanaging Placements:** Manually optimizing for specific placements (e.g., Feed vs. Stories) is less effective. Meta's AI determines the optimal mix, so providing diverse creative assets is more important [3].
- **Ignoring Creative Diversification:** Relying on a single creative or minor variations leads to faster creative fatigue and limits Meta's ability to optimize at scale [3].
- **"20% every 2-3 days" scaling rule:** While not entirely deprecated, Andromeda has made larger scaling safer due to improved AI optimization. However, reckless scaling without sufficient data can still be detrimental [13].
- **"Frequency > 3" as a universal kill signal:** While frequency remains a factor, Andromeda's better audience matching can sometimes push this number up without necessarily indicating fatigue, especially with diversified creatives [12]. The focus should be on declining performance metrics rather than just frequency alone [12].

## Open Questions

- **Precise CPA Movement Confidence Intervals:** While practitioners acknowledge CPA movement can be noise vs. signal, specific, universally agreed-upon confidence intervals for top operators are not widely published in the current literature. This area may require further A/B testing and internal data analysis for individual businesses.
- **Optimal Budget Allocation for Hard-to-Sell Niches:** For service-based or hard-to-sell niches where people are not actively searching and require education/trust-building, there's ongoing debate among practitioners whether manual warming (awareness campaigns, video views, retargeting sequences) is still strategic or if broad conversion campaigns with trust in Meta's AI are sufficient [6].
- **Consensus on Scaling Strategy for Multiple Winning Creatives:** When a test ad set yields several winning creatives, there's no single converged expert practice on whether to move only the top 2-3, move an entire group that seems to work together, or simply increase the budget within the test ad set and pause underperformers [6]. This highlights an area where human judgment and continuous A/B testing are still critical.
